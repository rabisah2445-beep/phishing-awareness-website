Protect Yourself from Cyber Threats

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a cybercrime where attackers impersonate legitimate organizations to trick individuals into revealing sensitive information such as passwords, credit card numbers, or personal identification details. These attacks typically come through email, text messages, or fake websites.

Why Should You Care?

Phishing attacks can lead to identity theft, financial loss, data breaches, and compromised personal or business accounts. In 2023, over 3.4 billion phishing emails were sent daily worldwide, making it one of the most common cyber threats everyone faces.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Warning Signs

  • Urgent or threatening language demanding immediate action
  • Generic greetings like "Dear Customer" instead of your name
  • Suspicious sender email addresses with slight misspellings
  • Requests for personal or financial information
  • Links that don't match the supposed sender's website
  • Poor grammar and spelling errors
  • Unexpected attachments or downloads

Types of Phishing Attacks

üìß Email Phishing

The most common type where attackers send fraudulent emails that appear to be from reputable companies. They often include malicious links or attachments designed to steal your credentials or install malware on your device.

üé£ Spear Phishing

Targeted attacks aimed at specific individuals or organizations. Attackers research their victims and craft personalized messages that are more convincing, often impersonating colleagues, executives, or trusted partners.

üêã Whaling

High-profile spear phishing attacks targeting senior executives and important decision-makers. These attacks often involve sophisticated social engineering and can result in major financial losses or data breaches.

üí¨ SMS Phishing (Smishing)

Phishing attacks delivered via text messages. These often contain urgent messages about account problems, package deliveries, or prize winnings with malicious links.

üìû Voice Phishing (Vishing)

Phone-based attacks where scammers impersonate legitimate organizations like banks, tax agencies, or tech support. They use social engineering to extract sensitive information during the call.

üõ°Ô∏è How to Protect Yourself

  • ‚úì Verify sender identities before clicking links or providing information
  • ‚úì Enable two-factor authentication on all important accounts
  • ‚úì Keep software and security systems updated
  • ‚úì Use strong, unique passwords for each account
  • ‚úì Be skeptical of urgent or threatening messages
  • ‚úì Hover over links to check URLs before clicking
  • ‚úì Report suspicious emails to your IT department or email provider
  • ‚úì Never share sensitive information via email or text
